## Legal and IRS filing requirements for individuals, LLCs, and S‑corps

### Entity classification and elections

Daisy’s LLC “taxed as an S‑corp” usually rests on a valid **Form 2553** election accepted by the IRS. The IRS Form 2553 page explicitly states it’s used by qualifying small business corporations **and LLCs** to elect S status. citeturn14search0turn0search1

A critical technical point: IRS instructions for Form 2553 state that an eligible entity making an S election that “meets certain tests” **is treated as a corporation as of the effective date** and **doesn’t need to file Form 8832**. This matters if you’ve worried about “LLC → 8832 → 2553” sequencing. citeturn16view0

S‑corp eligibility basics are formal (shareholder limits, eligible shareholder types, one class of stock, etc.), and the IRS instructions list core tests (e.g., 100 shareholder cap; eligible shareholder types). citeturn16view0turn15search12

### Required federal returns and major deadlines

An S‑corp must file **Form 1120‑S** if it made a Form 2553 election, the IRS accepted it, and it remains in effect. citeturn12view0

Key date: IRS instructions state calendar-year S‑corps file Form 1120‑S by **the 15th day of the 3rd month** after year-end; for the 2025 calendar-year return, the instructions explicitly note the due date is **March 16, 2026**. citeturn12view0

S‑corp shareholder delivery: the 1120‑S instructions state the corporation must prepare and provide **Schedule K‑1** to each shareholder, and K‑1 must be provided by the date the 1120‑S is due (including extensions). citeturn12view0

Individual due date: IRS Topic 301 states **the 2025 Form 1040 due date is April 15, 2026** (general rule + weekend/holiday adjustment). citeturn14search7

### The S‑corp owner compensation rule (reasonable salary) and distributions

The IRS rule is straightforward, and the difficulty is documentation:

The IRS states: S corporations **must pay reasonable compensation** to a shareholder-employee for services **before** making non-wage distributions, and it lists factors used in evaluating reasonableness (training/experience, duties, time devoted, what comparable businesses pay, etc.). citeturn24view1

The IRS also explicitly notes it has authority to **reclassify** shareholder payments from distributions to wages (making them subject to employment taxes), and its S‑corp guidance points to examples and cases where IRS successfully treated “distributions/loans” as wages. citeturn24view1turn24view0

Practical safeguard: create an annual “reasonable comp memo” (even a 1–2 page document) showing:
- Role and responsibilities
- Hours/time devoted (calendar evidence)
- Comparable wage benchmarks (e.g., salary surveys, job postings, industry comp)
- Wage vs distribution policy (how you decided the split)

The IRS addresses this specifically:

### Audit-risk factors and how to minimize them while maximizing deductions

You generally minimize audit risk by making your return **internally consistent**, **industry-plausible**, and **well-substantiated**—not by “claiming less.”

High-signal risk factors for your profile:
- Wage/distribution imbalance (very low wages with high distributions), because IRS stresses reasonable compensation and can reclassify distributions to wages. citeturn24view1turn24view0
- Vehicle and travel deductions without contemporaneous logs/receipts. IRS substantiation rules are formalized in regulations (26 CFR § 1.274-5) and summarized in Pub 463. citeturn2search6turn2search2
- Home office deduction without meeting the “exclusive and regular use” style requirements and without defensible square footage/expense support; IRS provides a simplified method option and Pub 587 explains methods. citeturn2search3turn2search15
- “Unreimbursed employee business expenses” approach for shareholder-employees: Notice 2025‑5 reiterates the TCJA suspension of miscellaneous itemized deductions (through tax years beginning before Jan 1, 2026), which blocks many employee-expense deductions on the individual return for 2025. This makes an accountable-plan reimbursement approach much more important for S‑corp owners. citeturn3view0

Risk-minimizing safeguards that preserve deductions:
- **Accountable plan**: reimburse the shareholder-employee for substantiated business expenses rather than paying personally and hoping for a deduction; this aligns with the substantiation framework in Pub 463 / 1.274‑5. citeturn2search2turn2search6
- **Separate business bank/credit cards** + clean bookkeeping; Pub 583 is literally built around recordkeeping systems. citeturn17search0turn17search3
- **Reconcile books to tax**: Schedule L (balance sheet), Schedule M‑1 (book-tax reconciliation), and M‑2/retained earnings should tie to bookkeeping reality; errors here are common DIY failure points.
- **File on time**: late 1120‑S penalties can be material even when no tax is due at the entity level. IRS instructions state the late filing penalty for returns with no tax due is **$255 per month (up to 12)** multiplied by number of shareholders, and a separate penalty exists for failure to furnish correct K‑1 information (**$340 per K‑1**). citeturn13view1turn12view0

- Substantiation rules: Pub 463 and 26 CFR § 1.274‑5. citeturn2search2turn2search6  

Use a lightweight professional review if this is your first DIY year with an S‑corp. A 1–3 hour CPA/EA review focused on:
- Reasonable comp memo quality (IRS factors) citeturn24view1  
- Schedule L/M‑1 tie-out integrity
- Health insurance and payroll W‑2 treatment citeturn24view1  
often costs far less than full prep yet knocks down major error risk.

If you use any paid preparer help, get an engagement letter and remember responsibility: IRS Topic 254 says a paid preparer must sign and include their PTIN, but you’re ultimately accountable for the return’s information. citeturn31search1

## Time/skill required and cost–benefit estimate

### Effort estimate (first DIY year vs steady-state)

Assuming a single-owner S‑corp with reasonably normal transaction volume:

- Bookkeeping cleanup & reconciliation (largest variance): **6–25 hours**
- Payroll review / officer comp analysis / W‑2 health insurance handling: **3–10 hours**
- Prepare 1120‑S + K‑1(s) + depreciation & tie-outs: **8–20 hours**
- Prepare 1040 with K‑1, QBI (8995), health insurance (7206), basis (7203 if needed): **4–12 hours**
- State returns (unknown state): **2–10 hours**

**Total first-year DIY range:** ~**23–77 hours**, with a realistic “most people who are careful” band at **~25–60 hours**.

The NSA survey suggests that “forms-only” averages (1120‑S ~$903; 1040 $220–$323) are far below $2,000, implying your current accountant might be doing significant cleanup/advisory or handling complexities. citeturn30view1turn30view0

### Residual risk (what you’re “buying” with a CPA)

The financial stakes of being late or materially wrong are not theoretical:
- Late 1120‑S: IRS instructions state **$255 per month per shareholder** (up to 12 months) when no tax due, plus other penalty mechanics when tax due. citeturn13view1  
- Failure to furnish correct K‑1 information: **$340 per K‑1** penalty described in 1120‑S instructions. citeturn13view1  
- Misclassified wages vs distributions: IRS explicitly reserves reclassification authority and frames reasonable compensation expectations. citeturn24view1turn24view0  

So the “residual risk” in DIY isn’t an audit boogeyman—it’s **deadline + classification + substantiation discipline**.
